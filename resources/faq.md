# ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

## What does a PM intern actually do?

Day-to-day varies by company and team, but common activities include:

- Talking to users and synthesizing feedback into feature ideas
- Writing product requirement docs (PRDs) or specs for a small feature or improvement
- Working with engineering and design to scope and ship a project
- Analyzing usage data to inform a decision or measure a launch's impact
- Presenting findings or recommendations to the team at the end of the internship

Most internships give you ownership of one well-scoped project rather than broad responsibility across the whole product — the goal is to show you can drive something from idea to shipped outcome in a short window.

## How do I get PM experience as an engineering student?

You don't need a "PM" title to build PM-relevant experience:

- **Lead a technical project end-to-end** — even a class project counts if you can talk about the decisions you made, not just the code you wrote.
- **Take on product-adjacent roles in student orgs** — event planning, sponsorship strategy, and program leadership all involve prioritization, stakeholder management, and tradeoff decisions.
- **Build something with real users**, even a small one — a side app, tool, or website with actual usage data gives you a concrete story about prioritization and iteration.
- **Seek out PM-shadowing or cross-functional exposure** during technical internships — ask to sit in on planning meetings or user research sessions if your internship allows it.

The goal across all of these is the same: be able to tell a story about a decision you made, a tradeoff you weighed, and an outcome you drove.

## Do I need a CS/business degree?

No single degree is required. Successful PM candidates come from:

- **Engineering / CS backgrounds** — strong technical fluency, useful for technical products and TPM roles
- **Business backgrounds** — strong on market/strategy framing and stakeholder communication
- **Other backgrounds** (design, humanities, social sciences) — often bring strong user empathy and communication skills

What matters more than the degree itself is being able to demonstrate: user empathy, structured problem-solving, technical literacy (even if not a technical major), and the ability to influence without direct authority. Companies specifically value **diverse educational backgrounds** on PM teams because products serve diverse users.

## When should I apply?

- **Internships:** Most PM internship postings for the following summer open between **August and November**, but some open as early as May. Apply within the first 1–2 weeks of a posting going live when possible.
- **APM Programs:** Similar timing to internships — many open in the fall for the following year, though some (especially for new grads) recruit later.
- **New Grad roles:** Often open in fall for the graduating class, with some additional postings through spring.
- **General rule:** Earlier is almost always better. Many companies review applications on a rolling basis, so an application submitted in week one has a meaningfully better shot than the same application submitted in week six.
